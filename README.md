# DATA6550 - Privacy Audit Project

## Project Overview

This project examines real-world digital privacy practices by analyzing privacy policies from services that users actively engage with. Rather than studying privacy in theory, this project focuses on understanding how personal data is collected, used, and shared across different platforms.

The goal is to evaluate user rights, data practices, and regulatory compliance across multiple industries, including financial services, communication platforms, logistics, and entertainment.

## Objectives

- Analyze real privacy agreements accepted by users  
- Understand data collection and sharing practices  
- Evaluate user rights and opt-out mechanisms  
- Compare services across industries  
- Assess compliance with privacy regulations (GDPR, CCPA, etc.)  
- Identify best and worst privacy practices

## Methodology

The project follows a structured approach:

1. Service Identification  
Each member identified 8–10 digital services they actively use  

2. Service Selection  
3–4 services per member were selected for detailed analysis, ensuring diversity across industries  

3. Privacy Policy Analysis  
Each service was analyzed based on:
- Data collection practices  
- Data sharing policies  
- User rights  
- Opt-out provisions  
- Policy update procedures  

4. Comparative Analysis  
Findings were compared across services to identify similarities, differences, and patterns  

5. Regulatory Evaluation  
Privacy practices were evaluated against:
- GDPR  
- CCPA  
- VCDPA  

## Key Focus Areas

Data Collection

Data Sharing

User Rights

Privacy Controls  
Opt-out mechanisms and user settings  

Security and Compliance  
Encryption, legal protections, and regulatory alignment  

## Key Insights

Privacy practices vary significantly across industries. Financial services collect highly sensitive data under strict regulation, while communication and gaming platforms collect extensive behavioral data. Logistics services focus on operational data, and government services generally provide stronger legal protections.

Although most platforms provide some level of user control, opt-out mechanisms are often limited, and full data control is rarely possible. Privacy policies are complex and not commonly read by users, contributing to the privacy paradox.
